Understanding the Normal Distribution

Hey there! Today, we're going to explore the fascinating world of the normal distribution. It's like a magical shape that appears in many aspects of our lives. Picture this: you have a group of friends, and you measure their heights. If you were to plot all their heights on a graph, it would create a special curve that's wider in the middle and narrows towards the edges. This curve is what we call the normal distribution.

The normal distribution is unique because it helps us make sense of a bunch of data. In this case, most of your friends will be around the average height, which is the highest point on the curve. As you move away from the average towards the tall and short ends, the number of friends becomes smaller. This means that extreme heights, like really tall or really short friends, are less common.

Now, let's talk about two special friends: the Mean and the Standard Deviation. The Mean is like the superhero of the normal distribution. It's the average height of all your friends combined. The Standard Deviation, on the other hand, tells us how spread out your friends' heights are from the Mean. If your friends' heights are similar, the Standard Deviation will be small, and the curve will be narrow. But if their heights vary a lot, the Standard Deviation will be bigger, and the curve will be wider.

The normal distribution is super helpful in science, math, and many other fields. It allows scientists and researchers to understand patterns in data, make predictions, and solve all kinds of cool problems. So next time you see a bell-shaped curve or hear about the normal distribution, remember that it's like a secret code that helps us unlock the mysteries hidden in our data!

Anomaly Detection Algorithms

Isolation Forest

The IsolationForest 'isolates' observations by randomly selecting a feature and then randomly selecting a split value between the maximum and minimum values of the selected feature. More info here.

Pros:
  • Highly effective for detecting anomalies in high-dimensional data.
  • Does not require the number of anomalies to be specified beforehand.
  • Fast computation time.
Cons:
  • May produce false positives on multi-modal data.
  • May not work well on low-dimensional data.

Gaussian Mixture

A Gaussian mixture model is a probabilistic model that assumes all the data points are generated from a mixture of a finite number of Gaussian distributions with unknown parameters. One can think of mixture models as generalizing k-means clustering to incorporate information about the covariance structure of the data as well as the centers of the latent Gaussians. More info here.

Pros:
  • Can capture complex data distributions.
  • Works well on data with overlapping clusters.
  • Can be used for both clustering and anomaly detection.
Cons:
  • Requires the number of components (clusters) to be specified beforehand.
  • Computationally more expensive than some other methods.

One Class SVM

Unsupervised Outlier Detection. Estimate the support of a high-dimensional distribution. More info here.

Pros:
  • Effective for high-dimensional data.
  • Works well on small datasets.
  • Can handle non-linear data.
Cons:
  • Requires tuning of the kernel parameter.
  • May be sensitive to outliers in the training data.
